Pet Care Tips

Weather Effects

Set up multiple beds for your pet. Place them in places with varying levels of warmth and shade. This will train your pet to find necessary shade or warmth on their own.

Brush your pet's fur regularly. Groomed fur is important for maintaining your pet's blood circulation. Ungroomed fur leads to matts, which can trap heat in the summer or lead to frostbite in the winter.

Test if a surface or liquid is too hot or cold on your own skin. If the temperature is too much for a human, it'll be too much for your pet to touch. This is especially important when going for walks on hot pavement or snow!

Make sure your pet always has access to multiple sources of fresh, cool water. Add ice in the summer to keep the water cool. A heated bowl can help keep an outdoor water bowl from freezing outside during the winter.

Keep the indoor temperature below 26°C for short-haired pets and below 24°C if you have long-haired pets.

Add ice to your pet's water bowl throughout the day. You can also freeze a small balloon filled with water to make an ice sphere your pet can safely lick.

Make sure your pet has access to a cool shaded area. If you don't have an AC at home, check your nearby area for public areas like a park or even a mall that you can bring your pet too in case the weather gets too extreme.

Let your pet relax on warmer days. Exercise on a hot day can cause your pet's body to overheat and run the risk of heatstroke.

Keep the indoor temperature around 20-22°C, and no lower than 16°C. Install safety guards and covers around heaters and fireplaces to prevent your pet getting burned.

Use a humidifier to keep the air moist. In addition to reducing skin irritation, humidifiers help your pet breathe better by moisturizing their nasal passages. This can also prevent snoring!

Towel dry your pet's fur and check their paws after going for a walk in snowy conditions. During winter, your pet can step on hazards like ice, salt, and other chemicals. Before a snowfall, clear outdoor spaces of objects that would become unseen hazards underneath the snow.

Spend time outdoors when it is sunny. Exercise is important during winter, but be very careful not to spend much time outside, especially if your pet has conditions that are aggravated by the cold weather. A sweater or coat can help your pet stay comfortable longer, but don't force them to go outside if they're too cold.

If you pet is showing signs of Heat Stroke

  • • Take your pet somewhere cool and well-ventilated
  • • Offer them small sips of water but do not force them to drink
  • • Cool them down by rubbing cool (not cold!) water on them by hand, or draping a cool wet towel over them. Change the towel every five minutes.
  • • Call your vet asap who will be able to advise you further.

Signs of Heat Stroke

  • • Panting for cats, excessive panting for dogs
  • • Agitation, restlessness
  • • Dizziness
  • • Lethargy, weakness, lying down
  • • Reddened gums
  • • Increased heart rate
  • • Distressed breathing
  • • Vomit or Diarrhea, possibly with blood
  • • Little to no urine production
  • • Loss of consciousness

If your pet is showing signs of Hypothermia

  • • Take your pet somewhere dry and warm as quickly as possible
  • • Slowly wrap your pet in dry, warm blankets or towels
  • • Place bottles of warm water wrapped in blankets next to your pet
  • • Wrap your pet in warmed towels or blankets when transporting to the vet.

Signs of Hypothermia

  • • Shivering
  • • Dizziness
  • • Lethargy, weakness, lying down
  • • Blue gums
  • • Dilated pupils
  • • Distressed breathing
  • • Difficulty walking
  • • Loss of consciousness

If your pet is showing signs of Frostbite

  • • Take your pet somewhere dry and warm as quickly as possible
  • • Slowly wrap your pet in dry, warm blankets or towels
  • • Carefully soak affected areas in warm water, around 37-38°C or use warm water compresses.
  • • Once the area is warm, pat dry as thoroughly as possible.
  • • Wrap your pet in warmed towels or blankets when transporting to the vet.
  • • Do not use direct heat such as a hair dryer, heating pad, or hot water on affected areas as it can cause more damage.
  • • Do not massage or rub the affected areas

Signs of Frostbite

  • • Grey or blue discolored skin
  • • Affected area is painful to touch
  • • Cold or brittle feeling when touched
  • • Swelling
  • • Red, inflamed skin
  • • Shivering
  • • Distressed breathing
