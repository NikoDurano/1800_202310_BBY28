Pet Care Tips

Weather Effects

Set up multiple beds for your pet. Place them in places with varying levels of warmth and shade. This will train your pet to find necessary shade or warmth on their own.

Brush your pet's fur regularly. Groomed fur is important for maintaining your pet's blood circulation. Ungroomed fur leads to matts, which can trap heat in the summer or lead to frostbite in the winter.

Test if a surface or liquid is too hot or cold on your own skin. If the temperature is too much for a human, it'll be too much for your pet to touch. This is especially important when going for walks on hot pavement or snow!

Make sure your pet always has access to multiple sources of fresh, cool water. Add ice in the summer to keep the water cool. A heated bowl can help keep an outdoor water bowl from freezing outside during the winter.

Tips for a Hot Day

  • Keep indoor temperature below 26°C for short-haired pets and below 24°C if you have long-haired pets
  • Make sure your pet always has drinking water available, add ice to help keep it cool
  • Make sure you pet always has access to a cool shaded area
  • Restrict exercise on warm days
  • If a pavement is too hot to put your hand on, it’s too hot for your pet to walk on
  • You can trim longer hair (but do not shave your pet!)

Tips for a Cold Day

  • Keep indoor temperature around 20-22°C, and no lower than 16°C
  • Provide your pet with options for sleeping with varying levels of warmth
  • Keep your home humidified and towel dry your pet after coming inside
  • For short-haired animals, consider buying them a sweater or coat to wear outside
  • Arthritic and elderly pets may have more difficulty walking in snow, and short-legged pets can get colder faster if their bellies come into contact with snow covered ground
  • After walks, wash and dry your pet’s feet and stomach to remove ice, salt, and chemicals and check their paws for any cracks or redness
  • Antifreeze with ethylene glycol is lethal for cats and dogs even in small amounts. Clean up any antifreeze spills quickly and keep your pet away from de-icers.

If you pet is showing signs of Heat Stroke

  • Take your pet somewhere cool and well-ventilated
  • Offer them small sips of water but do not force them to drink
  • Cool them down by rubbing cool (not cold!) water on them by hand, or draping a cool wet towel over them. Change the towel every five minutes.
  • Call your vet asap who will be able to advise you further.

Signs of Heat Stroke

  • Panting for cats, excessive panting for dogs
  • Agitation, restlessness
  • Dizziness
  • Lethargy, weakness, lying down
  • Reddened gums
  • Increased heart rate
  • Distressed breathing
  • Vomit or Diarrhea, possibly with blood
  • Little to no urine production
  • Loss of consciousness

Signs of Hypothermia

  • Shivering
  • Dizziness
  • Lethargy, weakness, lying down
  • Blue gums
  • Dilated pupils
  • Distressed breathing
  • Difficulty walking
  • Loss of consciousness
